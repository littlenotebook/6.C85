Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

To read the graph, I first had to understand the legend at the top left. The text states that just the line represents 0 cases, and the red area represents 150k new Covid-19 cases in the United States. The graph has the spiral and twelve dashed lines. Annotations tell me that the dashed lines correspond to the months, where January is the vertical line and the months increase clockwise. The spiral begins in January, having an annotation for the year 2020, so I infer that this dataset starts from January 2020. As the spiral moves outwards, the red area increases, indicating that cases of Covid-19 are increasing. The fattest part of the spiral occurs around November 2020 to March 2021 and then thins. Then the red area begins to increase again around August 2021 to October 2021, staying consistent until a huge increase in Jan 2022, where the spiral ends. The end of the spiral likely means that this article was published soon after, so there was no additional data yet.

  • I see a trend where Covid-19 cases increase in the later half of the year, likely because the weather becomes colder resulting in more indoor gatherings. Moreover, seasonal sickness like the flu can weaken immune systems, causing more people to get sick easier.
  • The consistently thicker red area after July 2021 suggests a sustained period of time where a lot of people were getting Covid-19 every week, this might result from loosened regulations and weaker Covid-19 prevention measures as people believed that the risk was over.
  • This graph visualizes that Covid-19 spread very quickly, as I observe that the red area from October 2020 to November 2020 almost tripled in size. Moreover, the thickness of the spiral didn't vary much from start of the month to end of the month, suggesting that the people who got sick likely spread it to at least one other person very soon.

Design Critique:

The shape of the spiral isn't very conventional in temporal visualizations and adds some cofusion to any reader trying to understand the visualization. However, the spiral shape does condense a lot of information very concisely, into a small space, compared to a line graph that would be very horiziontally long over three years of data. The annotations are nicely placed, especially the years and the arrows pointing to the direction the reader's eye should look. These annotions are very necessary because we conventionally read up to down, left to right, and not around in circles. One question I had while first looking at the graph was if we could tell what day or week this data was for by looking at the spacing on the dotted lines, but this was not part of the design. I do understand the inspiration between the spiral, which probably is a clock. Since clocks are also structured with time increasing clockwise, it's reasonable that the author would design a temporal visualization this way.
Using the area around the spiral to represent a numerical value isn't a very effective visual encoding, as it's difficult for the audience to compare irregularly shaped areas. However, it helps that the area is outlined in a solid, dark red color, so the reader can look at the ups and downs of the line instead. The spiral shape also makes comparing the trends across the entire graph difficult, because the data is at different angles. For me, comparing month to month was easier than comparing year to year. While the legend is helpful to understand what the red area around the line means, the 150k value doesn't provide much 'context' to the reader. Personally, when I saw the number 150k, I thought, Oh that's a big number, but as I was reading the graph, the number never came to mind. I don't think this graph effectively communicates the scale of the data to the reader.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I wanted to change the spiral shape to a visualization type that would be easier to read, therefore I chose the classic line graph. To help visualize the change in new covid-19 cases over time, I set the months on the x-axis and the number of cases on the y-axis. I set the range to 0 to 500k, which I estimated was a suitable range based on the graph. I also split the y-axis into 5 intervals, which allows the reader to more accurately read the numbers. I also annoted which months belonged to 2020, 2021, and 2022.
  • I decided to but the dates on top, because that's how we usually read calenders. I believe putting the x-axis on top also makes the graph more spacious.
  • I wanted to make the trends month to month more distinguished so I added gray dotted lines separating each month. In this way, one could see both the yearly and monthly trends.
  • I kept the red color because red is eye-catching, and carries a more serious connotation than soft pink or greens.
  • This visualization creates a very wide output image, which might be hard to display on small screens or paper. To better fit it on a screen, it would be possible to add a horizontal sliding/scrolling function.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Instead of repeating the months in the first graph, I chose to just display January through December once on the x-axis. This time I put the x-axis on the bottom, because we are used to reading scatter plots from down to up. It makes sense to have the reader first read the month, and then read the data points that correspond to that month.
  • Different from sketch 1, I labeled the y-axis as average number of new Covid-19 cases per month. This is because a scatter plot can't show the distribution over the month like the line graph can, so I found another metric. I felt that finding the average wouldn't be misleading, because from what I observe from the original graph, there is small variation from start to end of each month.
  • In order to differentiate between the years, I used different colors. These colors are pretty distinguishable, and none of them are too bright.
  • This graph is more compact than the line graph, and it also allows the reader to easily compare trends for one month across different years. However, it doesn't show the trends within each month, so it's not as effective for showing how quickly Covid-19 spread.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I aimed to integrate both the continuous trends per month and the average number of new cases for comparison between 2020 and 2021. So I thought of using a comparison chart that uses line marks, because we are good at comparing lengths visually. As there are only two averages I can estimate from the original graph: total new cases over the year / 12, and average number of cases over a certain month, I only needed 2 line marks. Since the first is consistent for each month throughout the year, I decided to use a dashed mark to represent it, so it wouldn't overpower the later. The second average is more specific to each month, so I used a solid line to represent it. In this design choice, I chose to highlight the trends between months more than the trends between years.
  • I put the data for 2020 on the left, the names of the months in the middle because both years share the same months, and the data for 2021 on the left. I felt this was pretty readible and concise.
  • I wanted to keep the red areas from the original graph because they show how fast Covid-19 spread. To do this, I copied the shape of the red line/area and kept only the half above the line. I think having areas both above and below the line would get confusing and crowded, so I only kept the top half. Because each monthly interval is the same size, the red areas can no longer correspond to the actual number of cases, so they only show the trend. I think this retains the most important takeaway even without conveying the exact numbers, and even the original graph didn't really convey the exact numbers. Instead of making the reader guess, this design choice gets rid of area estimating and just shows the visual trend.
  • The legend explains what the lines mean, but I couldn't think of a way to explain the red areas. Since the red areas don't represent numbers anymore, I felt that maybe it was okay to not include it in the legend. Possibly some sort of annotion or text would help explain what the red area represents. X0k and X00k labels stand for the actual values, but I didn't calcualte them.
  • I added the gray striped background to help the reader distinguish between consecutive months.

Final Visualization Design

Final Writeup

I chose to build off of the line and area graph in sketch 1 for my final design. Unlike the data from the original NYTimes graph, this dataset includes the entire year of 2022. Therefore the tornado plot (sketch 3) wouldn't work anymore. Finally, I like the line graph more than the scatterplot, because it shows the trends within each month.
The aim of my visualization is to show the virulence of Covid-19, both how quickly the virus spread and how fatal it was. I chose the two measures to best represent this: New Confirmed and New Deceased. To show Covid-19's transmissiblity, I used a line graph to depict how New Confirmed cases changed from month to month. Condensing the data to be just year to year wasn't enough to show the nauances of the trends, and expanding the data to week to week elongated the graph until the trends were also hard to visualize. I added additional grid lines between the month in order to provide the readers an opportunity to clearly observe the trends within a month too if they wished. I also think additional grid lines help the reader compare and contrast the degree of increase or decrease better than without them.
Because the reader's eyes would likely focus on the biggest peak at Jan 2022, making the other years seem less important as a result and making it hard to visualizae the trends for all three years equally. To combat this bias, I added the log trend line of New Confirmed for each year. I chose log over linear because log could better summarize the up and downs across the years. I used a dashed a lighter gray line so it doesn't overpower the main line graph.
In order to show how fatal Covid-19 was, I also felt that adding New Deceased would help bolster my argument that Covid-19 was a dangerous virus. Because I didn't want to crowd the space with multiple lines, and I feel that newly confirmed cases were interconnected with newly deceased cases as spread of the virus causes more people to be at risk of dying, I decided to represent New Deceased without a separate line. Instead, I used the thickness and color hue of the New Confirmed line. I believe that using both thickness and color hue instead of only one element helped bring more visual emphasis on the New Deceased data. As the hue becomes more intense when the value of New Deceased gets big, this also helps to lead the reader's eyes to the areas of the graph where we see the biggest harm of Covid-19. Since neither axes show the scale of the New Deceased data, the legend on the right is important to understand what value different line thicknesess represent.
I also decided to add annotations to the data points at the two peaks. This helps the reader understand the scale of the data by seeing exact numbers, and it also might aid the reader in understanding how to read the graph. I considered that adding the values of New Deceased in the annotations might be confusing if someone misses the legend, since the numbers seem quite random and not on the same scale as the number of New Confirmed. However, I included it in the annotation anyway because having both New Confirmed and New Deceased values describes the data point as a whole. Hiding or not including information might affect this visualization's reliability or trust in the reader's perspective.
For further steps, I would've like to add some other line or visual element to provide more context to put into perspective how many people died from Covid-19. Data visualizations often take out the emotional aspect, but the Covid-19 pandemic affected a lot of people and families, I think more could be done to create emotion, empathy, and maybe even regret that this pandemic had multiple devastating periods because of weakening regulations too soon.

As I state in my original critique, I think my line graph visualization is more easily understandable to the reader, because people are used to reading chronologically from left to right, not in a spiral order. I also improved on the lack of precisness of the area under a line by adding annotions and multiple intervals on the y-axis. The way I added new data (New Deceased) by varying thickness of the line might cause exact valuesof New Confirmed being harder to read. Another critique that I want to add of the original NYTimes graph is that as I was trying to make sense of the trends, I was always trying to remember which months are between the four labeled months. So instead of making the reader ponder about which month comes before April, I labeled every month on the x-axis. As a result, my graph does take up more space and looks repetitive, but allows the reader to focus more on the trends of the line. However, the spiral graph is visually interesting and creative, while a line graph is pretty common and not unique.
